Summary

This tool provides the sample size calculations for desired precision or statistical “power”.



Sample Size for Precision

The Sample Size for Precision calculations provide the minimum required sample size, of a proposed cross-sectional survey which tests for HIV and follows up by testing for biomarkers of recent infection amongst HIV positives, to attain a desired precision (relative standard error) of the incidence estimate, given assumptions about

  • population prevalence and incidence

  • recency test characteristics and coverage

  • “design effects” for the precision of cluster based sampling estimates of a) HIV prevalence, and b) the proportion of ‘recent infections’ among HIV positives tested for recency.

 

Sample Size for Power

The Sample Size for Power calculations provide the minimum (common) sample size of two cross-sectional surveys (which each test for HIV and follow up by testing for biomarkers of recent infection amongst positives), to achieve a desired probability of detecting a difference in incidence (with the correct sign), given assumptions about

  • population prevalences and incidences (with the implied incidence difference to be detected)

  • characteristics and coverage of the recency test(s)

  • “design effects” for the precision of cluster based sampling estimates of a) HIV prevalence, and b) the proportion of “recent infections” among HIV positives tested for recency, for both surveys

 

The Sample Size for Power Calculator distinguishes three cases:

  • Case I: The two surveys used the same recency test, with a single estimate of MDRI and a single estimate of FRR to be used for analysis of all data

  • Case II: The two surveys used the same recency test, with a single estimate of MDRI, but with FRRs that are independently estimated in each survey context

  • Case III: The two surveys used independently estimated MDRIs (possibly for different tests, and different associated cut-off times T) and independently estimated FRRs

  

Crucial note on choice of null hypothesis, calculation of p-values and meaning of “power”

It is not uncommon for a null hypothesis to be underspecified until the final data is available, i.e. for certain details (such as a hypothetically shared value of a test statistic) to be specified (estimated) in light of the data. In the present situation, the hypothetically common value of incidence, as well as the (potentially different by context) values of prevalence, cannot be reasonably supplied without seeing the data. Once data is available, there is further no unique, obviously correct, approach to deriving these parameters, and different choices can be entertained. The simplest, but not necessarily optimal, (indeed potentially problematic) approach is to hypothesize that the two survey populations are identical in terms of incidence AND prevalence, as this suggests a naïve pooling of all data to provide the hypothetically common values. However, a simple test for difference of prevalence would be expected to lead to the rejection of the null hypothesis (of equal prevalence) in many, if not most, cases, as prevalence will be relatively precisely estimatable in any survey remotely powered to estimate incidence. Hence, a more flexible approach to the null hypothesis is required.

 

The approach taken in the present tool is to:

  • hypothesize that the incidence is the same in the two contexts

  • be agnostic on the matter of the value of this shared incidence

  • hypothesize that the estimated value of the variance of the incidence difference estimate (calculated carefully in accordance with the choice of how test properties are shared between the surveys) is a good estimate of the actual variance of the incidence difference. (This is equivalent to a routinely made assumption in less complex situations)

  • allow the prevalence for each context to be independently estimated from the data from that context. (Note that even if the data does not support the rejection of the hypothesis that the prevalences are identical, there is little or no advantage in constructing the null hypothesis to include equal prevalence.)

The preceding assumptions allow the calculation of a p-value, which is done according to a two-tailed test, capturing the point that the experimenter has no basis to pre-determine the acceptable direction of any incidence difference.

 

Note that while it is common to report “power” as the probability of correctly rejecting a null hypothesis, we are specifically insisting on a two-tailed test, where the direction of any inferred difference will specifically be either correct or incorrect. Hence, we formally calculate the probability of correctly detecting the correct sign of the incidence difference. Numerically, the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis, but with the incorrect direction for the inferred effect, will usually be very small, unless the study design has no useful statistical power in any case.

 

Worksheets in this Workbook

The Sample Size Calculator contains seven worksheets or tabs:

  • <Welcome to ABIE> (introduction to the ABIE toolbox in general and major release three)

  • <User Guide> (this document)

  • <Sample Size for Precision>

  • <Scenario Comparison>

  • <Sample Size for Power (I)>

  • <Sample Size for Power (II)>

  • <Sample Size for Power (III)> 

 

The sheets are described in more detail below.

 

<Sample Size for Precision>

Context:

A cross-sectional survey is planned, and expected prevalence and incidence values, estimates or hypothetical values of test properties (MDRI and FRR), and estimates of design effects are available. An appropriate sample size must be determined.

 

Utility:

This sheet calculates the minimum sample size consistent with the relative standard error (RSE) of the incidence estimate desired by the user.

 

Note:

  • Incidence is entered as an “instantaneous rate”, with units of % per annum (p.a.).

  • The minimum possible relative standard error of the incidence estimate is not in general zero, and depends in detail on the uncertainties (relative standard errors) of the recency test characteristics (MDRI and FRR), as well as the hypothetical prevalence and incidence. The minimum possible RSE of the incidence estimate is reported in the cell directly above the cell where the desired RSE is entered. An error message is displayed if the desired RSE is less than the minimum.

  • The calculation only takes into account those subjects whose HIV status is ascertained. It does not assume, however, that recency test results will be available for all HIV positive subjects. The percentage of HIV positive subjects for which recency test results are expected to be available – the recency test coverage – should be close or equal to 100%.

  • The output under the heading “Expected Survey Counts” gives the numbers of HIV negative subjects, HIV positive subjects, recency test results, and recency-test-positive subjects that would be expected if a survey were to be carried out with the minimum required sample size proposed by the calculator, in a context described by the inputs. Expected survey counts are also given for a survey carried out with a “user-defined sample size”, which may, for example, be a round number greater than the minimum required sample size.

  • The sheet displays a graph of the RSE of the incidence estimate as a function of sample size. The shape of the curve, including the minimum RSE, shown as a horizontal dashed asymptote, is determined by all of the inputs except the desired RSE. The desired RSE determines the point on the curve which corresponds to the minimum sample size. The graph is displayed for values of sample size from 0 to 50,000.

  

<Scenario Comparison>

Context:

As in the <Sample Size for Precision> sheet, but questions remain about one or more of the inputs (population incidence and prevalence, test characteristics or coverage, design effects, required precision).

 

Utility:

This sheet performs the same calculation as the <Sample Size for Precision> sheet, but allows for up to 10 sets of inputs, and thus, for example, for a sensitivity analysis indicating how sample size depends on the input assumptions or on desired RSE.

 

Note:

  • Each scenario is specified in a column much like column D in the <Sample Size for Precision> sheet.

  • It is envisaged that the main use of this sheet is to implement sensitivity analysis of minimum sample size modifying one input at a time; the 10 scenarios can however be selected arbitrarily and independently of each other.

  • It is recommended that the user at some point enter each input set into the <Sample Size for Precision> sheet in order to view relevant warnings and error messages.

 

<Sample Size for Power (I/II/III)>

Context:

Two cross-sectional surveys are to be carried out, in order to estimate the size of the difference in HIV incidence between the two populations. Expected prevalence and incidence values for the two populations, estimates or hypothetical values of the properties of the recency test(s) (MDRI and FRR), and estimates of design effects are available. An appropriate sample size (common to the two surveys), consistent with a desired probability of correctly diagnosing the direction of the incidence difference, must be determined.

 

Utility:

This sheet calculates the minimum required sample size, common to two surveys, consistent with the “power” (probability of inferring a difference in incidence, with the correct sign) desired by the user.

 

Note:

  • To simplify the calculation, it has been set up expecting that the higher of the two underlying incidence values is supplied in scenario I. An error message alerts users to the violation of this presumption.

  • The “power” as defined here has an upper limit which depends on the uncertainties (relative standard errors) of the recency test characteristics (MDRI and FRR), as well as the hypothetical incidence and prevalence. The maximum power is reported in the cell directly above the one where the desired power is entered. An error message is displayed if the desired power is greater than the maximum possible power.

  • The case where the two surveys are to have different sample sizes is not considered here. Nor is the case where one survey has already been done, and the question is how large a sample size is required of a second survey in order to reliably detect differences between 1) the scenario for which an estimate is already in hand, and 2) the scenario in which a survey is still to be performed.

  • Incidences are entered and reported as “instantaneous rates”, with units of % per annum (p.a.).

  • The calculation only takes into account those subjects whose HIV status is ascertained. It does not assume, however, that recency test results will be available for all HIV positive subjects. The percentage of HIV positive subjects for which recency test results are expected to be available – the recency test coverage – should be close or equal to 100% for both surveys.

  • The output under the heading “Expected Survey Counts” gives the numbers of HIV negative subjects, HIV positive subjects, recency tests performed, and recency-test-positive subjects that would be expected for each survey if the surveys were to be carried out with the minimum sample size in a context described by the inputs provided. Expected survey counts are also given for surveys carried out with a “user-defined sample size”, which may, for example, be a round number greater than the minimum sample size.

  • Each of these sheets displays a graph of “power” (the probability of correctly inferring an incidence difference with the correct sign) as a function of the common sample size. The shape of the curve, including the maximum power, displayed as a horizontal dashed asymptote), is determined by all of the inputs except the desired power. The desired power determines the point on the curve which corresponds to the minimum sample size. The graph is displayed for values of sample size from 0 to 50,000.
